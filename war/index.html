Methodology / The cycle of maintenance

The labor of gathering what has scattered.

Identity inside a catastrophe
Durational performance
2026

I trace what happens to identity inside a catastrophe that does not end. I was drawn to kintsugi as a method: the labor of gathering what has scattered, the impossibility of perfect fit, the visible evidence that something held together under pressure.

The action proceeded in cycles. A porcelain vase with blue underglaze painting was broken and reassembled weekly. Each cycle demanded a week of work: locating fragments, negotiating spaces, maintaining the conditions for repair. The fear of the first breaks gradually dulled into familiarity. A strange recognition that this, too, was now part of the routine.

Over time, the vase lost nearly a third of its original body. Shards flew with force into distant corners, under furniture, into unreachable places. The form remained, but only through continuous effort, through the accumulation of adhesive mass that distorted and expanded the original silhouette. The golden seams recorded the tension between the desire to keep the shape recognizable and the physical reality of irreversible change.

The final reconstruction required breaking a second object: a small plate with the same blue rim where, in Russian folklore, dreams arrive effortlessly. I buried those dreams in the vase's body, using its fragments to fill the gaps where original pieces were missing. This was the recognition that maintaining any coherence now demanded sacrificing what had once seemed given.

Others participated in the breaking and the mending. The action became a shared rhythm of maintenance, a collective holding-together that consumed time, labor, attention. It was the proof that certain forms of existence persist only through continuous, exhausting effort, and that survival looks less like triumph than like stubborn, altered continuity.

Object & Process

Specifications

The specific vessel may vary provided it maintains the blue-and-white ceramic aesthetic; crack patterns are unique to each iteration, with the density of accumulated gold seams serving as the primary visual marker of the object's history.

Process Protocol

The work exists as a durational performative action repeated over an extended period. Its physical trace is a porcelain vessel with traditional blue-and-white underglaze painting, approximately 40 × 20 cm.

Each cycle follows the same protocol. After breaking, fragments are collected. Some are inevitably lost, scattered beyond recovery. The remaining pieces are bonded using two-component epoxy resin mixed with metallic gold pigment. The reference to kintsugi here is functional rather than decorative: the visible seams record where the object has held together under pressure.

With each iteration, perfect reconstruction becomes physically impossible. Adhesive mass builds up, distorting the original silhouette. Volume expands, symmetry drifts, the surface accumulates irregularities. The object drifts into a new configuration shaped by loss and the labor of maintenance.

The process concluded when original material proved insufficient to maintain the vessel's form. A second object, a small plate with matching blue rim, was broken and incorporated. This final substitution marks the point where maintaining any coherence required sacrificing what had been taken for granted.

Materials

Porcelain vessel with traditional blue-and-white underglaze painting, two-component epoxy resin, metallic gold pigment.
